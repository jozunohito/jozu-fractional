The 11th Hour

How a $1.6M deal survived a free-OCR offer, an incumbent with shared board members, and a pitched 11th-hour battle to take the deal away. The story of holding the line without conceding.

The Method
"The most powerful Power Message is one based on a previously unconsidered need."
Power Message lives where you uniquely solve what the buyer needs and competitors cannot.

At 4:47 PM on a Wednesday, eight months and four onsite meetings into the deal, the email landed: "They're now offering OCR for free." The competitor was the incumbent patient-engagement vendor. They had board members in common with the buyer. They had spent eight months losing this deal. And now, in the 11th hour, they were torching their margin to take it back.

01It started as a $30K commodity.

Three or four vendors including jumbo OCR commodity players. Half a penny per page across 5.5 million faxed provider orders. Procurement-led, feature-checklist competition. An AE running a feature-led motion would have won or lost on accuracy benchmarks, booked $30K, and moved on.

02I sat in the call center and watched the deal change.

I scheduled the first meeting at 9:00 AM with the executives and sat alongside the call center reps. I watched and witnessed patients calling to get scheduled, only to be told their paperwork was still being keyed. They went straight to their doctors and got referred to a competing center. They were gone now.

Layer 4 surfaced the real problem. Imaging equipment underutilized. Every no-show cost measurable revenue. The drift attributable to intake-to-scheduling friction was far larger than the OCR budget. Layer 5: they were not buying OCR. They were buying revenue infrastructure that OCR was a component of: integrated intake, patient engagement, and scheduling on a single data spine.

03The Power Messages were built on what the buyer had now seen.

By the time we reached proposal, the deal was no longer about OCR. It was about revenue infrastructure. Every Power Message in the proposal was anchored to economic impact I had witnessed firsthand, validated by the customer. The compelling event was now the incumbent patient-engagement vendor's contract renewal, which was just around the corner.

"They're now offering OCR for free."

The 11th-hour message that ends most deals
04The moment most sellers concede.

4:47 PM on a Wednesday. The competitor blinked. The free-OCR offer was a desperate move to collapse the deal and use their existing margin to pay for OCR. This is where most sellers concede. They match the discount. They cut scope. They lose the deal even when they win it.

I went back to the Power Messages. I re-validated each one with the buying committee, in writing. I showed them the thread: imaging utilization, no-show recovery, scheduling throughput, top-line revenue impact. Every line built on the five-layer discovery they had walked through with me. The competitor's free-OCR offer addressed exactly zero of them. They saw the "free-OCR" offer as the ploy it was.

05The proposal held the line under pitched assault.

Three weeks of pitched battle by the other rep to take the deal away. My proposal outlasted every move. The buyer chose the platform over the price. $30K became $1.6M ARR. 53x expansion. Incumbent displaced against the headwind of shared board members.

What Most Sellers Miss

Power Messages survive 11th-hour price wars when they are built on the buyer's quantified pain, not a surface-layer value proposition. That distinction is most of what enterprise selling actually is.
